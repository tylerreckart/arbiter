# The Hour Before the House Wakes

The kettle ticks as water climbs toward heat.
Outside, a gutter drips its slow arithmetic
onto the patio stones, each drop
a small erasure of the night.

I stand in the kitchen's half-light,
wool socks on cold tile,
watching the window fog and clear,
fog and clear — my breath
making and unmaking a world.

The oak at the fence line holds
its last few leaves like second thoughts,
not ready yet to mean the thing
that falling means.

Somewhere upstairs a floorboard
settles into its old argument with the cold.
A door stays shut. The dark
behind it keeps its own time.

The kettle finds its note.
I pour. The steam rises
and disappears — not gone,
just changed into the room,
into the quiet that holds
everything before the day begins.
